The field
hazards.

Twenty-four ways an EdTech founder in Nepal gets killed — and what to do about each, from sales-cycle starvation to brain drain to the data-breach you didn't see coming.

Most Nepali EdTech startups don't fail on the idea. They fail on a known, named, predictable risk that the founder either didn't see, or saw and didn't respect. This volume names them, ranks them, and pairs each with a concrete mitigation.

§ 01 — The Risk Landscape

Six families, twenty-four risks.

Risks don't come randomly — they cluster into families. Treat them like that. Solve a family's root pattern and you neutralise several risks at once.

§ 02 — Family 01 · Market & Customer

When the buyer moves slowly.

Schools are the most patient buyers in Nepali B2B. Cash runs out at the speed of patience.

Risk 01 · slow-sales

Sales cycles eat the runway.

4–9 months from intro to signed contract is normal.

Schools meet five vendors, ask the same questions, defer to the next academic-year budget cycle, and only sign during a narrow window — typically pre-Baishakh (March–May). A startup that runs out of cash before the buying window dies whether or not the product was good.

Mitigation
  • Time fundraising so 18+ months of runway covers two academic cycles, not one.
  • Build a free or nearly-free tier that gets you live in schools during the off-cycle, so you're already on the inside when budgets reopen.
  • Sell add-ons to schools that already have an ERP — shorter cycle, no displacement battle.
Risk 02 · payment-delays

Schools pay 4–6 months late.

Receivables age faster than cash arrives.

Payment terms agreed on paper rarely match payment dates in reality. A school that owes ₨ 2 lakh in March may not pay until September. Multiply by twenty schools and your working capital evaporates while revenue technically grows.

Mitigation
  • Bill annually upfront with a 10–15% discount, not monthly arrears.
  • Automate payment: integrate eSewa / Khalti / ConnectIPS so parents pay through your gateway and you net out school fees from the float.
  • Tie product access to payment status — politely throttle features rather than chase invoices.
Risk 03 · concentration

Top three customers = half your revenue.

Lose one, lose the company's quarter.

Early-stage EdTech in Nepal is structurally concentrated — a handful of marquee schools account for most of the topline. Any one of them switching, deferring, or going through ownership change knocks out a quarter or more.

Mitigation
  • Track the "no-customer-over-15%" rule from year two onwards.
  • Don't customise heavily for any single customer — every custom feature deepens lock-in for them, not for you.
  • Build a smaller-school self-serve product as a long tail to dilute concentration.
Risk 04 · word-of-mouth

One angry principal, twenty lost deals.

Reputation transmits through closed networks.

Nepal's school-owner community is small and gossip-rich. PABSON, NPABSON, HISSAN groups talk. A single botched implementation or a publicised bug becomes a brand event. Conversely, one delighted principal becomes ten warm intros — but the asymmetry is brutal.

Mitigation
  • Over-invest in the first ten customers; they are your sales force whether you pay them or not.
  • Build a customer-success function from day one, not month thirty-six.
  • Have a public incident-response playbook so problems are handled visibly well.
§ 03 — Family 02 · Financial

When the capital doesn't come.

Nepal's startup capital pool is small, slow, and conservative. Plan for that, or plan to die.

Risk 05 · capital-thin

There is almost no Nepali EdTech VC.

Dolma, True North, One to Watch & a thin angel layer — that's it.

Indian, US, or Singaporean capital is reachable but adds Foreign Investment approval (DOI/NRB), longer diligence, and FX layers. Local family offices exist but rarely write meaningful EdTech cheques. The funding desert kills companies with otherwise viable products.

Mitigation
  • Plan to be cashflow-positive by month 24 — assume no Series A will rescue you.
  • Layer in services / consulting revenue early. Many successful Nepali tech firms started as agencies that productised.
  • Court grant capital — World Bank, ADB, USAID, FCDO, INGOs — for socially-positioned products like rural ERP or vocational platforms.
  • Consider revenue-based financing or convertible notes from local angels rather than priced rounds.
Risk 06 · burn-rate

The implementation team eats the SaaS margin.

Nepali schools need on-site setup; SaaS economics assume self-serve.

A "₨ 1 lakh/year ERP" looks profitable until you count the three-week onboarding, twice-yearly trainings, and a customer-success person who handles four schools at most. Real margin per customer is often negative for the first 18 months.

Mitigation
  • Charge a one-time setup fee (₨ 25k–75k depending on size) — non-negotiable, separate from subscription.
  • Industrialise onboarding into a 14-day playbook with checklists, video courses, and a dedicated implementation pod.
  • Push self-serve from day one, even if adoption is slow — every quarter you wait makes the unit economics worse.
Risk 07 · pricing-pressure

A race to the bottom on price.

Incumbents discount aggressively to defend market share.

When you knock on a Veda customer's door, the easiest defensive move is a 30% discount and a year's free support. Eight competitors mean prices keep falling. Founders who don't differentiate on something other than price become irrelevant.

Mitigation
  • Win on a clearly different value axis: outcomes, accessibility, niche segment, AI feature — not on rupees per student.
  • Bundle proprietary content / data so the price is for the bundle, not the software.
  • Don't compete head-on for the customers incumbents will fight hardest for. Go where they aren't.
Risk 08 · fx-and-policy

Currency & capital-flow surprises.

NRB rules can shift; remitting USD revenue isn't always simple.

If your business serves NRN diaspora or foreign B2B clients, paying contractors in Nepal involves bank conversions, FX paperwork, and rules that occasionally tighten. Founders who scale before sorting their entity structure get stuck.

Mitigation
  • Engage an NRB-experienced accountant from incorporation day.
  • If targeting overseas customers, consider a Singapore or Delaware parent with a Nepal subsidiary — done early, this is cheap; done late, it's a nightmare.
  • Keep at least 6 months of operating cash on shore in Nepal regardless of foreign banking arrangements.
§ 04 — Family 03 · Regulatory & Legal

The paperwork that bites.

Compliance failures rarely kill startups outright — they kill the next funding round, the government tender, or the acquisition.

Risk 09 · child-data

You're handling minors' personal data.

Nepal's Privacy Act & emerging data-protection law treat this strictly.

Student names, photos, attendance records, exam marks, biometric scans, parent contacts — every EdTech is a children's data company whether the founder thinks of it that way or not. A breach involving minors is a brand-ending event in Nepal as elsewhere.

Mitigation
  • Data-minimise from day one — collect only what the product needs, delete what it doesn't.
  • Encrypt at rest and in transit. Use cloud providers' default encryption; don't roll your own.
  • Run an annual third-party security audit even when nobody requires it. Make this a marketing point.
  • Publish a clear, plain-Nepali privacy policy — most schools and parents don't get one.
Risk 10 · ird-vat

VAT, TDS, and IRD audits.

Tax compliance is unforgiving and back-dated penalties are real.

SaaS billing in Nepal carries VAT obligations many founders discover late. TDS on contractor payments, advance tax, e-invoicing — the IRD has been digitising and tightening enforcement. Schools also expect IRD-compliant invoices from your platform.

Mitigation
  • Hire a Nepal-based CA from month one. Don't try to DIY this.
  • Build IRD-compliant invoicing into the product itself if you're billing schools — eases your customer's compliance and yours.
  • Keep separate books for any foreign-currency or grant revenue; it has different tax treatment.
Risk 11 · tender-lockout

Public tenders favour incumbents.

Past-performance criteria can structurally exclude you for years.

Government EdTech tenders — at MoEST, UGC, CTEVT, local-government level — routinely require 3–5 years of similar projects, ISO certifications, and minimum revenue thresholds. New entrants rarely qualify, even if they have the better product.

Mitigation
  • Win the private market first; tender for public work in years 3–5, not year 1.
  • Subcontract to a qualifying prime — you build, they bid. Less revenue, but real referenceable scale.
  • Target lower-government tiers (rural municipalities, ward-level) where past-performance bars are softer.
  • Pursue ISO 27001 / 9001 early; tenders increasingly check.
Risk 12 · ip-weak

IP protection is thin in practice.

Copying happens; legal recourse is slow and rarely worth it.

A competitor can clone your UI in weeks and a court case takes years. Patents are theoretically available but rarely defended successfully. Trade-secret protection depends on your own internal hygiene.

Mitigation
  • Treat speed and network effects as your real moat, not legal IP.
  • Tight NDAs and non-competes for engineers — and a culture worth staying in so they don't want to leave.
  • Trademark your brand name early; that's the one IP fight that does work in Nepal.
  • Keep proprietary data (item banks, content libraries, integration code) in modules you don't expose, not in the front-end.
§ 05 — Family 04 · Operational & Talent

When the team wobbles.

Nepal's biggest startup risk isn't the product or the market — it's keeping a team intact while the rest of the world tries to hire your engineers.

Risk 13 · brain-drain

Your best engineer leaves for Australia.

Or the US, the Gulf, or remote work paying USD.

This is the single biggest operational risk in Nepali tech today. A senior Nepali engineer can earn 4–8× more on a remote contract for a US firm than at a Nepali startup. Student visas to Australia/Canada accelerate the exit further. Losing two senior people in a quarter can stall product development for half a year.

Mitigation
  • Pay above local market — but accept you can't match foreign-remote rates. Compete on equity, mission, and growth.
  • Hire deliberately from outside Kathmandu (Pokhara, Biratnagar, Butwal); retention is structurally higher.
  • Build a documentation culture — assume any individual can leave at any time and the system has to keep running.
  • Take ESOP seriously; not as a gimmick. If equity is real, retention is real.
Risk 14 · founder-burnout

The founder running on fumes.

Year three is when most founders quietly break.

Long sales cycles, customer hand-holding, fundraising pressure, family expectations, and the lonely psychology of running a small company in a small market — these compound. Burnout in Nepal is under-discussed because admitting it carries social stigma.

Mitigation
  • Have a co-founder, not a solo build. Equity dilution is cheaper than collapse.
  • Find a peer group of three or four other Nepali founders; meet monthly. The conversations are the therapy.
  • Take a real holiday at least once a year. The company will not collapse in a week off; it will collapse in a year of you not having one.
Risk 15 · cofounder-conflict

Co-founders fall out.

The most common cause of small-company death globally — and Nepal is no exception.

Disagreements over direction, equity, work intensity, money, family — they accumulate. In Nepal's small ecosystem, a public co-founder split also damages future fundraising and recruiting.

Mitigation
  • Vesting schedules from day one — no exceptions. 4-year vest, 1-year cliff, even between best friends.
  • Written shareholders' agreement covering exits, deadlocks, and what happens if one founder wants out.
  • Quarterly "are we okay?" conversations, formally scheduled. Hard, but it works.
Risk 16 · scaling-ops

Customer support becomes a black hole.

Schools call directly, expect immediate help, in their language.

A Nepali school doesn't open a ticket. They call the founder's mobile, in Nepali, often during exam week, and expect the bug fixed today. As the customer base grows, this support load swallows the founder's time and then the engineering team's.

Mitigation
  • Build self-help: a Nepali-language help centre, video tutorials, in-app guidance — earlier than feels comfortable.
  • Hire support staff from teaching/admin backgrounds, not engineering. They speak the customer's language literally.
  • Train customers, not just users — a one-day onboarding workshop reduces support tickets by 60%.

In Nepal's tech market, a brilliant founder with a mediocre team will lose to a mediocre founder with a retained, documented, well-paid team. Hiring is product strategy.

§ 06 — Family 05 · Competitive & Tech

When someone better-funded shows up.

It might be Google with a free product, an Indian SaaS expanding northward, or a local incumbent that finally noticed you. Either way — assume they will.

Risk 17 · free-incumbents

Google & Microsoft give away your category.

Google Classroom and Teams are free, polished, and globally maintained.

For the LMS / online-class layer, you are competing with $0 from a company with infinite resources. Your school customers will quietly use both: your platform for ERP, Google Classroom for actual teaching. Anything you build at the LMS layer must beat free.

Mitigation
  • Don't compete head-on at the generic LMS layer. Win on Nepal-specific layers — language, NEB-aligned content, biometric integration, parent comms.
  • Integrate with Google Classroom rather than against it: import grades, sync rosters. Be the bridge, not the rival.
  • Make admin / Nepal-compliance the primary value, with light LMS as a feature, not the core.
Risk 18 · indian-saas

An Indian SaaS lands with venture money.

Camu, Teachmint, Classplus & cohorts have already eyed Nepal.

A well-funded Indian EdTech can move into Nepal with marketing budgets you can't match, hire your sales reps, and underprice you for two years to take share. Their disadvantage is local nuance — IRD compliance, Nepali language depth, government relationships.

Mitigation
  • Make Nepali-specific compliance, language, and integrations a deep moat — things they can't replicate without a Nepal team.
  • Lock in marquee schools with multi-year deals when threats appear.
  • Consider strategic partnerships rather than pure competition — a regional player needing local presence may acquire rather than compete.
Risk 19 · adjacent-entry

Banks & telcos move into your category.

Fee fintech is a natural extension for any Nepali bank.

If your wedge is fee-collection or payments, expect NIC Asia, Nabil, Global IME or eSewa itself to launch a school-fee product within 24 months. They have distribution you can't match — every parent already has the bank app.

Mitigation
  • Don't sit on the payments layer alone. Wrap payments inside a richer school workflow they won't build.
  • Partner with one bank early — be the application layer they distribute, not the rival.
  • Move up the value chain to academic management before the payments commoditise.
Risk 20 · breach

A data breach.

The single most catastrophic technical risk you face.

Student data, parent contacts, fee records, biometric scans — a leak ruins customer trust permanently. Even if you survive operationally, it ends the founder's reputation in the closed school-owner community for years.

Mitigation
  • Security is a senior-engineering responsibility, not a checkbox. Have a named security owner from team-of-five onwards.
  • Penetration testing twice a year by an external firm. Budget it.
  • Incident-response runbook ready before you need it: who calls whom, what gets disclosed, how parents are notified.
  • Cyber-liability insurance — small Nepali market for it, but the larger insurers offer policies. Get one.
§ 07 — Family 06 · Founder & Macro

The risks you don't control.

Earthquakes, elections, pandemics, currency moves. You can't prevent them — you can only build resilience for when they arrive.

Risk 21 · political

Government changes; education policy reverses.

A new minister, a new curriculum, a paused initiative — and three months of work shifts under you.

Nepal has had multiple education ministers in recent years. CDC priorities, NEB structure, school-grading systems — all open to revision. If your product depends on one specific policy direction, you're exposed.

Mitigation
  • Build for the underlying need (assessment, attendance, billing) not the specific regulation. Needs persist; rules don't.
  • Stay close to multiple stakeholders — MoEST, CDC, UGC — without depending on any one.
  • Diversify across school types so a policy change in one segment is partial damage, not total.
Risk 22 · disasters

Earthquake. Pandemic. Floods. Strikes.

Nepal's exposure is real and recurring.

2015 earthquake; 2020 COVID lockdowns; recurring monsoon floods; political bandhs. Schools close, payment systems pause, your team can't get to office, your servers can't be reached. Each event is survivable — being unprepared isn't.

Mitigation
  • Cloud-host with offsite backup outside Nepal. Don't run critical infra on a single Kathmandu data centre.
  • Remote-first work culture — proven in COVID, still useful. The team should be functional from anywhere.
  • Keep 6 months of cash buffer specifically tagged as "disaster runway", separate from operating reserves.
  • A documented business-continuity plan that's been rehearsed at least once.
Risk 23 · econ-downturn

Schools cut IT spend in a slow economy.

EdTech is the first line item to defer when fees are short.

A weak remittance year, a banking-sector wobble, or rising inflation → schools delay upgrades, parents default on fees, your churn rises and new sales slow. EdTech revenue is more cyclical than founders typically believe.

Mitigation
  • Position as cost-saver in tough times: "We reduce your admin headcount cost by X." Not "we are an upgrade."
  • Have annual contracts with auto-renewal so a downturn affects new sales but not existing book.
  • Diversify revenue: institutional + consumer + government grants spread the cycles.
Risk 24 · founder-network

No network in the school-owner community.

In Nepal's relationship-driven B2B, the cold founder loses.

Founders without family or social access to PABSON / NPABSON / HISSAN circles spend years building what insiders inherit. Cold pitches to principals work, but at 5–10% the conversion of warm intros.

Mitigation
  • Recruit one co-founder, advisor, or first-hire who has those relationships — or marry the function into a senior sales hire.
  • Become a presence at industry conferences (PABSON conventions, principal training events). Showing up consistently for a year creates the network.
  • Offer free product to the most-respected school in your segment in exchange for case studies and intros. The reference is worth more than the revenue.

§ 09 — Risk Cascade

How one risk becomes three.

Risks rarely arrive solo. A late payment delays salary, salary delay triggers a senior departure, departure stalls product, stall causes a customer to churn. The cascade is the real killer — track it.

§ 10 — Priority

If you only fight three.

Twenty-four risks is too many to attend equally. Founders who do this well attack three relentlessly and accept proportional preparation on the rest.

i.

Capital scarcity (R05).

Without money, none of the other twenty-three matter. The Nepali EdTech founder who plans for cashflow positivity by month 24, layers in services revenue, courts grant capital, and never bets on an external round to rescue them — that founder survives.

Family 02 · Financial
ii.

Brain drain (R13).

Your second-best engineer moves to Sydney; your senior product person joins a US-remote contract. Documented systems, ESOP that's actually meaningful, hiring outside Kathmandu, and a culture worth staying in are not soft assets — they are the difference between continuing and quietly ending.

Family 04 · Operational
iii.

Child-data breach (R09 / R20).

A trust event that ends a brand permanently. Every other risk has recovery paths; this one closes them. Treat security as a senior engineering responsibility, run third-party penetration tests, and prepare an incident-response runbook before you ever need it.

Reading this Brief

This is the third volume of the Nepal EdTech series. Vol. 01 catalogued the incumbents. Vol. 02 mapped the gaps. Vol. 03 names the hazards facing anyone who tries to fill those gaps. The three documents work as a system — read together, they produce a complete strategic view of the market for an entrepreneur preparing to enter.

Severities here are heuristic, not measured. A founder's actual risk profile depends on segment, capital, team, and timing — use the framework, but do the assessment for your specific case.
