§ 01 — The Buying Journey

Who actually decides — and when.

Nepali schools don't buy software the way SaaS companies assume. Five actors are involved, at different stages. Getting the sequence wrong wastes months.

§ 02 — The Sales Calendar

When Nepal buys.

There are two real buying windows per year. Miss them and you wait another half-year. The calendar is not a metaphor — it is the single most important operational artefact for your sales team.

§ 03 — Ideal Customer Profiles

Four archetypes, four playbooks.

Don't sell to everyone. Pick one ICP for the first 18 months and get it right before expanding. A school at ICP-A and a school at ICP-D need completely different conversations.

Mid-large private boarding school, Kathmandu Valley

500–1,500 students. English-medium. Owner-operated. Has tried paper-based systems, maybe attempted an earlier ERP that failed. 3–5 admin staff. Board of trustees approves annual spend. Principal has real influence but owner signs the cheque.

§ 04 — Channel Partner Ecosystem

You can't cold-call your way in.

Nepali school procurement is relationship-mediated. The fastest route to a deal is through a trusted intermediary the school already knows. Map your channels before you hire your first sales rep.

Tier 1 · Most Important

School Associations — PABSON, NPABSON, HISSAN

The fastest warm-intro machine in Nepali education. An endorsement from a PABSON district chapter opens doors no cold-call ever could. Attend their annual conventions. Sponsor one session. Get on the vendor list. This is where your first 20 customers come from.

  • Annual national convention + district-level meetings
  • Vendor booths — ₨ 20–50K investment, 40+ principal conversations
  • Training session sponsorships (your product = the demo)
  • HISSAN chapter meetings for +2 college access
Tier 1 · Most Trusted

CA & Auditor Referrals

Every school has a CA. The CA sees the pain first — fee defaults, IRD notices, manual ledgers. A CA who recommends your platform carries far more authority than any sales rep. Build a formal referral program: 10–15% of first-year ARR for a signed customer.

  • Formal reseller / referral agreement with quarterly payouts
  • Co-branded IRD-compliance pitch decks for their school clients
  • CA training workshop on your accounting module
  • Accountancy Institute partnerships for lead flow
Tier 2 · Scale Channel

ISP & Hardware Vendors — Worldlink, Vianet

ISPs already sell to schools and have trusted relationships for billing and support. A bundled offer ("₨ 3,000/month: broadband + school ERP") reduces your CAC to near zero. Hardware vendors (biometric device sellers) are the same — schools buying fingerprint scanners need software that works with them.

  • ISP bundled offer — internet + ERP on one invoice
  • Biometric device reseller partner agreement
  • Laptop/tablet vendor co-sell for rural school device access
  • Revenue share: 8–12% of SaaS year-1 revenue
Tier 2 · Rural Unlock

District Education Offices & INGOs

For the thousands of community and public schools that Veda and e-School will never reach, INGOs (Room to Read, Save the Children, World Education, CARE Nepal) and District Education Offices are the distribution layer. They already have trust; you provide the product.

  • DEO-endorsed pilot for one district → referral to all schools in district
  • Grant-funded deployment: INGO pays per-school cost, you build the install base
  • Matching-fund programs (school pays 30%, INGO covers 70%)
  • OLE Nepal as integration partner for offline-rural hybrid
Tier 2 · Embedded Revenue

Payment Gateways — eSewa, Khalti, ConnectIPS

eSewa and Khalti want more transactions. Schools are large, recurring transaction sources. A co-sell deal — "use our ERP, collect fees through eSewa at 0% school commission" — creates a zero-cost sales channel while letting the gateway subsidise your customer acquisition.

  • eSewa / Khalti school programme: marketing + no-fee-collection deal
  • Gateway-funded implementation for high-volume schools
  • Data sharing on fee-default patterns (with privacy compliance)
  • Co-branded "digital school" campaign
Tier 3 · Long-term Brand

EdTech Media — EduSanjal, Principal YouTube

EduSanjal is the primary English-language education media platform in Nepal. Principal-educator YouTube channels (in Nepali) have meaningful reach in the school community. Sponsored case-study videos from satisfied principals function as both marketing and social proof.

  • EduSanjal sponsored feature / product review
  • Principal testimonial videos in Nepali (YouTube + WhatsApp forwarded)
  • PABSON newsletter sponsored content (low cost, high trust)
  • Teaching-community Facebook groups (admin staff communities)
§ 05 — The Sales Motion

Seven touches, seven weeks.

Every deal follows roughly this sequence. The talk-tracks are real — these are the phrasings that actually work with Nepali school owners and principals. Never lead with product; always lead with pain.

1
Week 1  ·  Warm Intro

The peer introduction.

You don't cold-call a principal in Nepal. Someone they know — a fellow principal, their CA, their PABSON chapter chair — introduces you at an event or over the phone. Your job at this stage is a single sentence that gets you a 20-minute tea meeting.

What you say
"Hajur ko school ma fee collection ra attendance ko kaam kasari hudchha? Hamro system le yesto school haru lai dhherai maddat gareko chha — ek palta heraun?"
2
Week 2  ·  Discovery Tea

Ask about their pain. Don't demo.

Sit down, drink tea, ask questions. What keeps them up at night? Fee defaults? Exam-mark entry? Parent complaints? Identify the single loudest pain. Only show that one module. Leave a one-page overview in Nepali and the name of a reference school they can call.

Key questions
"Last year ma fee default rate kati thiyo? … Exam result nikalne kati din lagchha? … Parent complaint kasto aauchha?" — never ask "what software do you use?"
3
Week 3  ·  The Demo

Show only what hurts them.

30-minute demo, in person, on a laptop. Show only the module that matched their pain. Use real school names (with permission). Show the parent app on your phone. Let the admin staff ask questions — they're the daily users. End with "What did you not see that you expected?"

Demo rule
"Aaja hami fees module matra herauchhaun — kinabhane hajurle bhannu bhaeko fee default nai sabai bhandha thulo samasya ho. Arko kehi herna parchha bhane aagaami pali heraulaun."
4
Week 4  ·  Reference Visit

Let a happy principal close for you.

Arrange a 30-minute call or visit between your prospect and your happiest similar-sized school. Don't attend. Let them talk freely. The questions they ask each other — "does it really reduce fee defaults?" "how long did setup take?" — are things no salesperson can answer credibly.

Reference framing
"Ek jana principal chhan — Kathmandu Valley maa, hajur ko school jasto size nai — unle phone ma 15 minute kura garnu huncha. Ma connect garauchu?"
5
Week 5–6  ·  Proposal

One page. No feature list.

A proposal is not a brochure. One page: the pain you discussed, how your platform solves it, the implementation timeline, and the annual investment. No per-feature pricing tables. No 30-item comparison matrix. A Nepali school owner should be able to read it in 3 minutes and decide.

Proposal format
Problem → Solution → Timeline → Investment. One side of A4. In Nepali if ICP-C/rural; bilingual for ICP-A/B. Never send as a PDF attachment — walk through it in person.
6
Week 6–7  ·  Negotiation

They will ask for a discount. Have a plan.

Nepali school owners negotiate everything. Expect a 20–30% ask. Never discount the subscription — discount the setup fee instead, or add a free module. This preserves your ARR while giving them a win. Offer 10% off for payment within 15 days of signing. 15% for 2-year commit.

Negotiation anchor
"Setup fee ma hami 50% discount dina sakchhaun — yedi hajurle Chaitra bhitra sign garnu bhayo bhane. Annual subscription ma chahi change garna sakdainau — arko school sanga same rate rakhnu parcha."
7
Day 1–14  ·  Onboarding

Live in 14 days or it never gets done.

The riskiest moment is between signing and going live. A school that takes 4 months to onboard churns at 3× the rate of one that goes live in 14 days. Send an implementation squad day one. Don't leave until admin staff can complete the three critical daily tasks without calling you.

The 14-day promise
"Sign gare ko 14 dina bhitra hajur ko school fully live hunchha — attendance, fees, ra parent app — natra 3 mahina free extension dinchhau. Yo hamro guarantee ho."
§ 06 — Pricing Architecture

Price for the market that exists, not the one you wish for.

Four tiers across the school-size spectrum. The "Standard" tier is your volume engine. The "Enterprise" tier funds the company. The "Seedling" tier builds the install base. Never lead with features — lead with school size and pain intensity.

Tier 0 · Up to 150 students
Seedling
Free forever
  • Student register & attendance
  • Basic fee record (no gateway)
  • Exam marks entry & report card
  • 1 admin user account
  • Community support only
  • No parent app · No payroll
Most popular
Tier 1 · 150–800 students
Standard
₨ 45 /student /yr
  • Full SIS + admissions CRM
  • Fee collection + eSewa/Khalti
  • Attendance + biometric ready
  • Exam, marks & report cards
  • Parent app (Android + iOS)
  • SMS + in-app notifications
  • 5 admin users
  • Phone + chat support
Tier 2 · 800–2,000 students
Pro
₨ 38 /student /yr
  • Everything in Standard
  • IRD-certified accounting
  • Payroll & HR module
  • Transport with GPS option
  • Library & hostel
  • Analytics dashboard
  • Unlimited admin users
  • Dedicated CSM
Tier 3 · 2,000+ / Multi-campus
Enterprise
Custom quote
  • Everything in Pro
  • Multi-campus consolidated view
  • Custom integrations + API
  • White-label parent app option
  • SLA: 99.9% uptime guarantee
  • On-site implementation team
  • Named account manager
  • Quarterly strategic review
Setup Fee (all tiers)
₨ 25K – ₨ 80K one-time

Covers data migration, staff training, parent-app onboarding, and 14-day implementation guarantee. Non-negotiable — but can be discounted as a closing lever (not the subscription).

Discount Policy
10% / 15% / never on subscription

10% off for annual upfront payment within 15 days. 15% off setup fee for Chaitra closing. 15% for 2-year commit. Never discount the per-student rate — it spreads and erodes your entire ARR.

Co-op / INGO Pricing
5-school cluster: ₨ 20/student/yr

A cluster of 5 small schools (avg 200 students each) shares one licence + one part-time CSM, billed as a co-op. INGO can subsidise 50–70%. Opens the bottom-of-market without destroying pricing integrity.

Billing Timing
Annual upfront, Baishakh invoicing

Invoice in Chaitra (March/April) before the new academic year. Schools have budget at this point. Monthly billing creates collection problems. Annual upfront gives you the working capital to fund operations.

§ 08 — The Numbers That Matter

Twelve KPIs, no vanity metrics.

Track only what tells you whether the business is healthy or dying. Three categories: Sales Engine, Product Health, Financial. Review weekly for Sales Engine; monthly for the rest.

Sales Engine
Sales cycle length by ICP
Target: ICP-A ≤ 90 days

The only number that tells you if your sales motion is working. If ICP-A takes 180 days, the intro quality is wrong or the demo isn't landing.

Sales Engine
Demo-to-proposal conversion
Target: ≥ 60%

Below 60% means your discovery is weak — you're demoing the wrong thing to the wrong pain. Stop demoing more. Ask better discovery questions.

Sales Engine
Proposal-to-close rate
Target: ≥ 35%

Below 35% means the proposal doesn't match what the principal actually cares about, or the reference check is missing. Not a negotiation problem.

Sales Engine
Customer acquisition cost (CAC)
Target: ≤ 1× first-year ACV

Anything above 1× ACV means you're buying customers, not acquiring them. Push channel partnerships — they drop CAC dramatically vs. direct sales.

Product Health
Day-14 activation rate
Target: ≥ 80% of schools fully live

The best predictor of renewal. A school that isn't live in 14 days has a 3× churn risk versus one that is. Track this for every new customer.

Product Health
Daily active admin rate
Target: ≥ 5 days/week per school

If an admin logs in only on report-card day, you're a report-card printer, not a platform. Daily use = switching cost = retention.

Product Health
Parent app activation
Target: ≥ 60% of parents within 30 days

Parent app adoption is your virality and your renewal lever. If parents love the app, the principal gets pressure to keep the subscription from parents — not just from you.

Product Health
Support ticket volume per school
Target: ≤ 3 tickets/month by month 3

More than 3 tickets/month in month 3+ means onboarding failed. Don't hire more support. Fix the onboarding.

Financial
Annual recurring revenue (ARR)
Target: ₨ 50L by month 18

This maps to ~100 Standard-tier schools at avg 200 students. The benchmark for Series A conversations with local funds (rough, as ecosystem is thin).

Financial
Net revenue retention (NRR)
Target: ≥ 110%

NRR over 100% means your existing customers grow faster than they churn. You hit 110% by upselling modules — payroll, transport, library — to already-live schools.

Financial
Gross margin per school
Target: ≥ 55% by year 2

Year 1 margin will be negative or near zero due to implementation costs. Year 2 should be 55%+ as schools self-operate and support volume drops. Below 55% = pricing or ops problem.

Financial
Accounts receivable age
Target: ≤ 30 days avg.

The most under-tracked number in Nepali SaaS. If your receivables age past 90 days, you're lending, not selling. Annual upfront billing and auto-throttling solve this before it becomes a crisis.

Field Rule · § 05–08

In Nepal, a demo closes a meeting. A reference school closes a deal. A delighted admin staff at an existing school closes your next ten deals. Invest your best people in the schools you already have.

§ 09 — Field Rules

The cheat sheet.

Compressed from conversations with school owners, principals, and the few EdTech founders who have navigated this market and survived. Follow these exactly.

Do this.

  • Lead with pain, not product. Open every meeting with "Tell me how you currently handle [X]." Don't touch the laptop for the first 20 minutes.
  • Show up at PABSON/HISSAN events every year, without fail. Presence at two events creates more pipeline than six months of cold outreach.
  • Give one school free for a year in exchange for a reference. Your reference school is your most valuable marketing asset. Invest in it.
  • Guarantee Day-14 go-live in writing. This differentiates you from every competitor who has burned schools with 4-month rollouts.
  • Send your Nepali-language collateral via WhatsApp. Principals don't open email attachments. They open WhatsApp forwards from trusted contacts.
  • Keep the proposal to one page. A 12-page RFP-style document signals you don't understand the buyer.
  • Build with your first five customers, not for them. Their feedback is your product roadmap. Put them on a weekly call.
  • Discount setup fee, never subscription. Protect ARR integrity from day one; you can't undo per-student pricing erosion.

Don't do this.

  • Cold-call a principal's mobile directly. It works 5% of the time and ruins your reputation in the 95%. Get an intro first, always.
  • Reach out during Dashain or Tihar. Even if you need the deal. A message sent during festivals communicates disrespect and gets ignored.
  • Do a full-platform demo before you understand their pain. Showing everything confuses and overwhelms. Show the one thing that hurts them most.
  • Compete on price against incumbents. Veda will match any discount you offer and win on trust. Beat them on something they can't copy.
  • Promise custom features to close deals. Every custom commitment becomes a support debt. Close on the standard product or don't close at all.
  • Leave onboarding to the school itself. Schools that onboard themselves fail. Send someone. Your retention rate depends on it.
  • Target government schools as your first customer. Long procurement cycles and approval chains will drain a new company's runway before a PO appears.
  • Build for the Kathmandu Valley alone. Valley competition is intense; your lowest-CAC and highest-retention schools are often outside the valley.
