The cracks
& the openings.

Where the fourteen incumbents fall short, why their business strategies leave money on the table, and what an entrepreneur can build into the white space.

A market with 900+ schools on one platform looks crowded. A market where no one has solved offline learning, teacher tooling, vocational tracks, parent literacy, or non-Nepali language education is wide open. Both are true.

§ 01 — Where the Service Design Breaks

Twelve cracks in the current stack.

These aren't edge cases. They are the recurring complaints from administrators, teachers, students, and parents who actually use these systems day to day.

Language & Localisation

English-first interfaces in a non-English country.

Most ERPs ship with English-default UIs. Rural admin staff and teachers struggle. Parent apps push notifications in English to parents who don't read English. Nepali support is often a half-translated layer, and other home languages — Maithili, Bhojpuri, Tharu, Newari — are absent entirely.

Only OLE Nepal and Sikai Chautari treat language seriously. None of the commercial ERPs do.
Connectivity

Designed for cities with reliable internet.

Almost every commercial platform assumes always-online. In hill and mountain districts, internet drops daily. Schools lose attendance records, can't run online classes, can't process fees during outages. There is no major commercial offline-first / sync-when-online option.

OLE Nepal proved offline EdTech works at scale — but it's a non-profit, not a commercial product.
Pedagogy Gap

ERPs treat learning as an afterthought.

Veda, e-School, Nimble & co. excel at billing and admissions. Their "LMS" is usually a file uploader plus a Zoom link. Real learning features — adaptive practice, formative assessment, learning analytics — are largely absent.

No Nepali platform meaningfully combines a strong ERP with a strong LMS in one product.
Teacher Tooling

Teachers are treated as data-entry users.

Lesson planning, curriculum mapping, rubric design, peer observation, professional-development tracking — none are seriously supported. The teacher's workflow is "log in, mark attendance, enter marks, log out." That's a clerical job, not a teaching job.

A teacher-first product would have zero direct competition in Nepal today.
Parent Engagement

Parent apps are one-way notification feeds.

Fee reminder. Attendance alert. Holiday notice. Repeat. There is no two-way conversation, no parenting support, no progress dialogue, no early-warning interventions for struggling kids. Parents become passive recipients, not partners in learning.

Parent literacy & engagement is the highest-leverage unaddressed lever in Nepali education.
Assessment Depth

Marks in, marks out — no insight in between.

Exam modules record numbers and produce report cards. They don't generate item analysis, identify learning gaps, suggest remediation, or flag students at risk. Online exams lack proper proctoring, anti-cheating, or honest pedagogy beyond MCQs.

Assessment-as-a-service is an entire product category waiting to be built.
Vocational / TVET

Almost nothing for the technical & vocational sector.

CTEVT-affiliated institutes train hundreds of thousands annually in trades, hospitality, IT, agriculture. Their workflows are different — practical assessments, internships, industry placements. Mainstream school ERPs don't fit. The TVET stack is largely manual.

Nepal's TVET sector is officially prioritised but commercially unserved.
University Depth

Beyond e-Zone, universities are underserved.

Research management, thesis tracking, plagiarism detection, alumni engagement, internationalisation, exchange programs, faculty research output — the higher-ed long tail is barely touched. e-Zone covers EMIS/FMIS at the regulator level; nothing covers the modern research university.

Universities import expensive foreign tools (Turnitin, Mendeley, Blackboard) for what local products could do.
Accessibility

Special education and disability are an afterthought.

OLE Nepal's Sanketik Sikai is the only platform meaningfully addressing sign language. Screen readers, dyslexia-friendly typography, ADHD-aware UI, autism accommodations — basically absent. WCAG compliance is rarely tested.

Accessibility-first EdTech could win moral authority and government partnerships.
Interoperability

Every platform is a walled garden.

Schools using two systems can't make them talk. NEB results don't import cleanly into all ERPs. Government EMIS doesn't sync with private school systems. There's no Nepali equivalent of OneRoster, no API standard, no common data schema.

Interoperability infrastructure is unglamorous but the lack of it slows the entire sector.
Analytics

Systems of record, not systems of insight.

Dashboards exist; useful predictions don't. Which students are at risk of dropping out? Which teachers' classes consistently underperform? Which fee-defaulters are likely to leave? Where is learning loss concentrated? None of this is surfaced — data sits in tables.

Predictive analytics is a thin layer on existing data — high-leverage, low-build.
Pricing & Access

Small schools are priced out.

Per-student SaaS pricing means a 200-student community school can't afford Veda or e-School. Their only options are free government tools (limited features) or doing nothing (the most common choice). The bottom 60 % of the market is unserved.

A genuine freemium tier or co-op model would unlock thousands of small institutions.
§ 02 — Where the Business Strategy Limps

Eight strategic blind spots.

It's not just the products. The way these companies sell, price, distribute and partner has structural flaws that an entrepreneur can systematically exploit.

Sales Motion

Relationship-driven, not product-led.

Most vendors close deals via principal/owner relationships, not via product trials. There is no self-serve onboarding, no free 30-day pilot, no viral parent-app loop. New entrants with product-led growth could leapfrog the incumbents' sales advantage.

Pricing

No freemium, no co-op.

Per-student SaaS suits big schools. It excludes small ones. There's no shared-cost model where ten village schools pool one ERP licence, no "free for under 100 students" tier, no government-subsidised ladder.

Capital

Bootstrapped, so under-built.

Almost no VC has flowed into Nepali EdTech. Most products are funded by services revenue. That keeps R&D thin and prevents the kind of UX investment global players make. A funded entrant could out-build everyone.

Customer Success

Onboarding takes months and often fails.

A typical school takes 3–6 months to fully roll out an ERP — many never finish. There's no playbook, no white-glove implementation team, no first-90-days outcomes guarantee. Customer success is treated as a cost centre.

Distribution

Direct sales only — no channels.

No reseller network. No teacher-college partnerships. No district-education-office bundles. No NGO distribution. Every sale is one-by-one, which caps growth at the speed of the founder's calendar.

Foreign Competition

Google & Microsoft eat the LMS layer for free.

Google Classroom and Microsoft Teams are free to schools and pay no Nepali tax. They quietly dominate the "online classes" use case. Local LMS players can't compete on price; they have to compete on Nepal-specific features and integration.

Government

Public tenders favour the incumbents.

Big government EdTech tenders tend to land with the same one or two vendors — often e-Zone — because of past performance requirements. New entrants are effectively frozen out unless they partner with incumbents or win at the local-government layer first.

Product Roadmap

Reactive features, not opinionated design.

Most vendors build whatever the loudest customer asks for. The result: bloated, unfocused products. There is no Nepali Linear, no Nepali Notion — no opinionated, design-driven product that says no to features. That's an opening.

§ 05 — Eight Concepts

What an entrepreneur could actually build.

Each concept ties to a specific gap above. Pitch line, who it's for, why now, why it can win. Pick one — don't try to build all of them.

Concept 01
HotB2B

Shikshak

A teacher-first OS — lesson planning, rubrics, peer learning, PD tracking.

Veda runs the school; Shikshak runs the teacher. Curriculum-aligned lesson templates, shared rubric library, classroom observation logs, and a peer-learning feed where teachers across schools swap what works. Sells per-teacher (₨ 200/month) into schools that already have an ERP.

Solves
Teacher tooling vacuum; PD invisibility
Buyer
Principal · Academic Coordinator
Beachhead
50–100 ambitious mid-tier private schools
Moat
Network effects from peer content
Concept 02
HotB2B2C

Abhibhavak

WhatsApp-first parent engagement that meets parents where they are.

Forget app downloads. A WhatsApp Business stack that the school plugs into its ERP, sending personalised progress nudges, fee reminders, parenting tips and 2-way Q&A — in Nepali, Maithili or English depending on parent profile. Voice notes for low-literacy parents. Solves the one-way notification problem at the channel parents actually open.

Solves
Parent engagement; literacy gap
Buyer
School (subscription) · or D2C parents
Beachhead
+2 colleges fighting fee defaults
Moat
WhatsApp BSP cost + content library

DeepB2B

Pahunch

Offline-first ERP for hill / mountain / community schools.

§ 06 — Service Design Patterns

Six service-model innovations.

Beyond product features — these are ways of delivering the service that no one in Nepal does well. Apply any of them on top of any concept above and the offering becomes hard to copy.

i

Outcomes-as-a-service, not features.

Sell the result, not the software. "We will get your NEB pass rate to 90% or your fee back." "We will reduce your fee-default rate by 30%." This shifts the conversation from RFP-style feature checklists to outcomes — and lets you charge premiums no feature-vendor can match.

A +2 college pays a per-student bonus for every distinction grade above baseline.

ii

Co-op pricing for small schools.

A cluster of 5–10 small schools pools an annual fee that gets them shared access to the platform plus one shared admin officer who supports all of them. Per-school cost drops 70%. The bottom-of-pyramid market opens up. The vendor builds a moat through relationships in the cluster.

Five community schools in Sindhupalchok share one ERP licence + one part-time admin.

iii

Two-week implementation guarantee.

Onboarding is the silent killer of EdTech ROI. A vendor that promises and delivers a fully-rolled-out system in 14 days — versus the industry's 3-to-6-month average — wins on word of mouth alone. Build a dedicated implementation squad, not a part-time CSM team.

"Day 14, parent app live with all students enrolled, or we extend free for 6 months."

iv

Bundled hardware + software.

Schools shouldn't have to source biometric devices, fingerprint scanners, ID-card printers and tablets separately. A vendor that ships the whole kit pre-configured, on a 3-year lease that bundles the software fee, removes a huge buyer-side friction and locks in the customer.

"₨ 50K/year per school: software, biometrics, parent-app branding, on-site setup."

v

Multi-channel by default — app, SMS, voice, WhatsApp.

Most products force every parent into the app. A meaningful chunk of Nepali parents won't or can't download an app. A service that automatically reaches each parent through the channel they actually use — voice IVR for low-literacy, SMS for feature-phone, WhatsApp for the rest — has wider engagement than any app-only competitor.

Same fee-reminder gets sent as voice in Tharu to one parent, WhatsApp in Nepali to another.

vi

Data sovereignty as a selling point.

All Nepali student data hosted in Nepal, processed under future Nepali data-protection law, audited locally. As compliance regimes tighten globally, "your data never leaves the country" becomes a real procurement argument — especially for government and university buyers who cannot use foreign-hosted alternatives at all.

A government tender that can't legally use AWS-hosted Google Classroom; you can win it.

§ 08 — Things That Will Bite You

Six reality checks.

Every opportunity above comes with a known failure mode. Founders who go in blind fail; founders who go in with eyes open survive.

Schools buy slowly.

Sales cycles of 4–9 months are normal. Decisions are made by a principal/owner who sees five vendors a year and trusts the one their friend's school uses. Plan capital for the gap.

Government tenders aren't a path.

Big public-sector EdTech contracts cluster with established vendors. Past-performance criteria can lock newcomers out. Win a private market first; tender later.

Free competitors set the floor.

Google Classroom & Microsoft Teams are free, polished, and globally maintained. Don't try to out-feature them at the LMS layer. Win on Nepal-specific layers they can't copy.

Implementation eats the margin.

SaaS economics assume self-serve. Nepali schools need on-site setup, training, hand-holding. Either price implementation in, or industrialise it — but never assume customers will figure it out alone.

D2C EdTech has thin LTVs.

Student subscribers churn after exams. Per-user revenue is small. Pure D2C without an institutional channel rarely reaches scale in Nepal — pair it with school partnerships from day one.

Talent is the bottleneck, not ideas.

Nepali product designers, ML engineers, growth marketers are scarce and expensive. Most EdTech failures come from execution, not strategy. Hire and retain ruthlessly — or partner with a stronger team early.
